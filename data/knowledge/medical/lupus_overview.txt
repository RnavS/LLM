Lupus, usually referring to systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), is a chronic autoimmune disease in which the immune system attacks healthy tissues in different parts of the body.

It can affect the skin, joints, kidneys, blood cells, brain, heart, and lungs. Common symptoms include fatigue, joint pain, joint swelling, skin rashes, mouth sores, fever, and sensitivity to sunlight.

The course of lupus can vary widely. Some people have milder disease with flare-ups and calmer periods, while others have more serious organ involvement.

Lupus nephritis is one possible complication of lupus, but lupus itself is not the same thing as nephritis. Lupus is the broader autoimmune disease, and lupus nephritis refers specifically to kidney inflammation caused by lupus.

Diagnosis usually depends on a combination of symptoms, physical examination, blood and urine testing, and clinical judgment by a licensed clinician.

Treatment depends on severity and which organs are involved. Management may include anti-inflammatory treatment, immune-modifying medication, monitoring for organ involvement, and long-term follow-up with a clinician.
