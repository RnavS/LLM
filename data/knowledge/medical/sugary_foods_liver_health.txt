Occasional candy such as Skittles is not known for a unique direct toxic effect on the liver in an otherwise healthy person. The bigger issue is repeated high added-sugar intake over time, especially when it drives excess calorie intake, weight gain, high triglycerides, insulin resistance, or type 2 diabetes.

Diets high in added sugar, especially frequent sugar-sweetened foods and drinks, can contribute to metabolic dysfunction and nonalcoholic fatty liver disease. That means the liver starts storing excess fat, which can sometimes progress to inflammation and liver injury if the pattern continues over time.

For most people, a small occasional serving of candy is more of a long-term dietary pattern issue than an immediate liver injury issue. The risk is higher when the person already has obesity, prediabetes, diabetes, elevated triglycerides, fatty liver disease, or heavy overall added-sugar intake.

Seek medical care if there are signs of liver trouble such as jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, persistent vomiting, severe abdominal pain, unusual confusion, or marked swelling.
