Hypertension means persistently elevated blood pressure. It often has no obvious symptoms, which is why it is sometimes called a silent condition. Over time, uncontrolled high blood pressure can increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, and other complications. Educational discussion of hypertension usually includes lifestyle factors, monitoring, and clinician-guided treatment rather than self-diagnosis.

Fever is generally a sign that the body is responding to infection, inflammation, or another stressor. A mild fever can happen with many common illnesses. The context matters: age, immune status, other symptoms, duration, and severity all affect how concerning a fever may be. Severe symptoms, confusion, dehydration, trouble breathing, or rapidly worsening illness can make a fever more urgent.

Dehydration happens when the body loses more fluid than it takes in. Common signs can include thirst, dry mouth, reduced urination, dark urine, dizziness, or weakness. Severe dehydration can become dangerous. Persistent vomiting, inability to keep fluids down, fainting, or signs of shock need urgent medical attention.

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory airway condition that can cause wheezing, cough, chest tightness, and shortness of breath. Symptoms may worsen with triggers like exercise, allergens, smoke, or infection. Severe breathing difficulty can be an emergency.

Diabetes mellitus is a group of disorders involving problems with blood glucose regulation. Type 1 diabetes involves loss of insulin production, while type 2 diabetes commonly involves insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency. Long-term management usually involves monitoring, lifestyle measures, and clinician-guided treatment plans.
