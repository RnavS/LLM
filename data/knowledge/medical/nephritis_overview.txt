Nephritis is inflammation of the kidneys. It can involve the glomeruli, the tubules, or the surrounding kidney tissue. Clinicians often use the term glomerulonephritis when the inflammation mainly affects the glomeruli, which are the filtering units of the kidney.

When the kidney filters or surrounding tissue become inflamed, the kidneys may remove waste, extra salt, and excess fluid less effectively. That reduced filtering can contribute to swelling, high blood pressure, blood or protein in the urine, and worsening kidney function.

Common symptoms of nephritis can include swelling in the face, hands, feet, or ankles; foamy urine from protein loss; blood in the urine; reduced urine output; high blood pressure; fatigue; and flank or back discomfort. Some people have only mild symptoms at first, while others develop more obvious changes in urination and swelling.

Nephritis can be caused by infections, autoimmune disease, medication reactions, or other immune system problems. Examples include lupus nephritis, post-infectious glomerulonephritis, and interstitial nephritis related to medicines.

Evaluation often includes urine testing, blood work, blood pressure checks, and sometimes kidney imaging or a kidney biopsy. Treatment depends on the cause and may involve stopping an offending drug, controlling blood pressure, treating an infection, or using immune-suppressing therapy under clinician supervision.

Severe swelling, very low urine output, severe shortness of breath, chest pain, or sudden confusion should be treated as urgent medical warning signs.
