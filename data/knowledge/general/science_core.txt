Photosynthesis is the process plants, algae, and some bacteria use to convert light energy into chemical energy. In simplified terms, carbon dioxide and water are transformed into glucose and oxygen. Chlorophyll helps capture light, especially in the blue and red parts of the visible spectrum. Photosynthesis mainly occurs in chloroplasts. The light-dependent reactions produce energy carriers, while the Calvin cycle helps build sugars from carbon dioxide.

The scientific method is a structured way to investigate questions. A typical cycle includes observation, question formation, hypothesis generation, testing, analysis, and revision. Good scientific work tries to reduce bias, uses careful measurement, and allows results to be replicated. A single experiment rarely proves something permanently; instead, confidence grows when different lines of evidence point in the same direction.

Atoms are made of protons, neutrons, and electrons. The number of protons determines the element. Electrons occupy energy levels around the nucleus and are central to chemical bonding. Molecules form when atoms bond in stable arrangements. Chemical reactions rearrange atoms rather than creating or destroying them under ordinary conditions.

Plate tectonics explains the large-scale movement of Earth's outer shell. The lithosphere is broken into plates that move slowly over geological time. Their movement helps explain earthquakes, mountain building, volcanic activity, and ocean trench formation. Convergent boundaries bring plates together, divergent boundaries pull them apart, and transform boundaries slide past one another.

The water cycle describes how water moves through the environment by evaporation, condensation, precipitation, infiltration, and runoff. Solar energy drives much of the cycle. Water can be stored temporarily in oceans, soil, glaciers, lakes, rivers, and the atmosphere.
